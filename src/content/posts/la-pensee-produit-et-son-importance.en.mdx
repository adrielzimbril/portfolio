The road to a great customer experience can be riddled with potholes. How do you navigate such a treacherous path? One of the main ways is to think about the product.

We know that customer experience is at the heart of every product. We are familiar with the [seven dimensions](https://drawbackwards.com/blog/7-cx-dimensions) that underpin customer experience, and we understand that what constitutes a good customer experience ultimately depends on [the eyes of the beholder](https://drawbackwards.com/blog/what-is-customer-experience-really): your customers.

But the journey toward a great customer experience can be fraught with obstacles. How do you navigate such a challenging route? One of the key strategies is to adopt a product-oriented mindset.

## What is Product Thinking?

Product thinking answers the question, “What makes your product useful?”

People buy products to solve real problems. Product thinking helps you identify what those problems are and how your product addresses them.

By employing product thinking, we view the product holistically rather than focusing solely on its features. We aim to solve our customers’ problems with meaningful solutions that deliver satisfaction and genuine value.

## How is Product Thinking Different from Design Thinking?

[Design thinking](https://drawbackwards.com/approach) asks what features will make your product as useful as possible. It is an iterative process that continuously addresses detailed questions about the appearance, feel, and functionality of the product. But without the product itself, there are no features to design.

Product thinking can provide a more comprehensive view of a product than design thinking. It captures the overarching purpose of why your product exists in the first place. It is the shared goal behind all your actions as an organization. It explains why you do the work you do.

In design thinking, we create many maps. Empathy maps and journey maps help us orient ourselves within the product development process and identify where we need to go next.

Maps are useful for showing you where you are and how to get there. Product thinking is like a globe that provides a model of the entire world you are mapping.

Product thinking and design are in constant communication. To extend the metaphor, product thinking steers the ship, while design thinking is in the engine room, providing feedback to the captain about what’s working or not, so the vessel can move in the right direction.

Product thinking is not reserved for product managers

It is easy to assume that maintaining a strategic and holistic perspective on your product is solely the responsibility of the product manager and business leaders. But that is not enough.

For a product to succeed, all team members must develop their skills in product thinking. It is almost impossible to create excellent products without everyone understanding what the product means for customers and why it exists in the first place.

> **This means that product thinking is a mindset that must be cultivated and reinforced. It is a customer experience culture that drives every decision.**

Everyone in the organization needs this mindset and commitment to support a common goal. This is because everyone in the organization shapes the customer experience in one way or another.

Like most things in business, leaders are often responsible for deciding and defining the vision and objectives. Product thinking is the connective tissue that links this broader vision to the detailed decisions made through design thinking.

## Why is product thinking important and how to do it?

Product thinking is essential to good UX design because it prevents us from creating things nobody wants. The goal of product thinking is to ensure that we focus on the complete user experience, not just a collection of interesting features.

Without product thinking, it can be easy to get lost in creating good design and experiences and forget the main reason why customers hired your product in the first place.

Here are three basic steps to ensure you think first in terms of the product and then of features:

1. **Identify the customer and their problem to solve.** You need to be sure you are solving a real problem that real people need to address.
2. **Discover the tasks for which the product is hired.** You must understand why you are building the product and how it will meet your customers' fundamental needs.
3. **Identify the desired outcomes.** Ultimately, you need a vision of what you want to achieve with the product and the features that will help realize that vision.
