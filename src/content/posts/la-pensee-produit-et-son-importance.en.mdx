The road to a great customer experience can be riddled with potholes. How do you navigate such a treacherous path? One of the main ways is by embracing product thinking.

We know that customer experience is at the heart of every product. We’re familiar with the [seven dimensions](https://drawbackwards.com/blog/7-cx-dimensions) that are central to customer experience, and we understand that what makes a great customer experience is [ultimately in the eye of the beholder](https://drawbackwards.com/blog/what-is-customer-experience-really): your customers.

But the journey to a great customer experience can be full of obstacles. How do you steer through such a risky road? One of the key approaches is to think in terms of the product.

## What Is Product Thinking?

Product thinking answers the question, “What makes your product useful?”

People buy products to solve real problems. Product thinking helps you identify what those problems are and how your product addresses them.

By using product thinking, we see the product as a whole rather than focusing solely on its features. We strive to solve our customers’ problems with meaningful solutions that deliver both delight and real value.

## How Is Product Thinking Different from Design Thinking?

[Design thinking](https://drawbackwards.com/approach) asks which features will make your product as useful as possible. It’s a cyclical process that continually answers detailed questions about how the product looks, feels, and works. But without the product itself, there are no features to design.

Product thinking can provide a more holistic view of a product than design thinking. It’s the big picture of why your product exists in the first place. It’s the shared purpose behind all your combined actions as an organization. It’s the reason you do the work you do.

In design thinking, we create a lot of maps. Empathy maps and journey maps help us orient ourselves to where we are in the product development process and where we need to go next.

Maps are useful for showing you where you’re headed and how to get there. Product thinking is like the globe that gives you a model of the entire world you’re mapping.

Product thinking and design thinking are in constant communication. To mix metaphors, product thinking steers the ship while design thinking is in the engine room, giving the captain feedback on what’s working (or not) to keep the boat moving in the right direction.

## Product Thinking Isn’t Just for Product Managers

It’s easy to assume that maintaining a strategic, holistic perspective on your product is solely the responsibility of the product manager and business leaders. But that’s not enough.

For a product to succeed, everyone on the team needs to hone their product thinking skills. It’s nearly impossible to create great products unless everyone understands what the product means to customers and why it exists in the first place.

> **This means product thinking is a mindset that must be cultivated and reinforced. It’s a culture of customer experience that drives every decision.**

Everyone in the organization needs this mindset and commitment to champion a shared purpose. That’s because everyone in the organization shapes the customer experience in some way.

As with most things in business, leaders are often tasked with deciding and defining the vision and purpose. Product thinking is the connective tissue that links this broader vision to the detailed decisions produced by design thinking.

## Why Is Product Thinking Important and How Do You Practice It?

Product thinking is essential to good UX design because it keeps us from building things nobody wants. The goal of product thinking is to ensure we focus on the complete user experience, not just a collection of cool features.

Without product thinking, it’s easy to get lost in creating good design and experiences and forget the main reason customers “hired” your product in the first place.

Here are three basic steps to make sure you’re thinking in terms of product first, and features second:

1. **Identify the customer and the problem to solve.** You need to be sure you’re addressing a real problem that real people need solved.
2. **Discover the jobs the product is hired to do.** You need to know why you’re building the product and how it will meet your customers’ fundamental needs.
3. **Identify the desired outcomes.** Ultimately, you need a vision for what you want to achieve with the product and the features that will help you realize that vision.
